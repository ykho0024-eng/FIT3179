Personal Safety in Australia

Understanding Violence Patterns Across States and Demographics

Personal safety remains a fundamental aspect of wellbeing, reflecting how secure people feel in their daily lives and relationships. This visual narrative explores trends in physical and sexual violence in Australia, using national survey and crime data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS). Through a series of visualisations, we uncover how experiences of personal violence have evolved over time, vary across states, and differ by gender, relationship, and demographic factors.

National Overview

This section provides a broad picture of personal safety across Australia. It highlights total reported offences, victimization rates, and national trends over time, helping establish the scale and direction of personal safety concerns.

Population-adjusted rates mirror total cases, indicating real behavioral shifts rather than demographic changes.

The national trend shows a complex picture of personal safety in Australia. After a period of increase, rates have stabilized in recent years, suggesting potential impacts of policy interventions and societal changes.

National data shows overarching trends that form the foundation for understanding Australia's safety landscape. To see what drives these numbers, the next section explores how different offence types contribute to overall personal safety.

Offence Type Composition

Here we break down offences by type to identify which forms of personal violence are most prevalent—such as assault, sexual offences, and robbery. This view clarifies where the greatest safety risks lie.

Dramatic variations exist across Australian jurisdictions:

  • Northern Territory: Consistently highest rates (>450 per 10,000)
  • Queensland: Apparent spike reflects data reporting changes, not actual crime surge
  • Western Australia: Steady deterioration with rates nearly doubling over 25 years
  • Victoria: Lowest-risk state with stable performance
  • ACT: Notable improvement since 2007 peak

Some offence categories stand out as major contributors to personal safety challenges, revealing the kinds of harm most commonly experienced by Australians. To understand where these offences are most frequent, we next compare offence cases by type and state.

Offence Cases by Type and State

This section examines how offence patterns differ across states and territories. Comparing regions helps uncover localized safety issues and the social or environmental factors that may shape them.

Assault dominates, comprising 70-80% of all offences throughout the timeline.

Steady growth from mid-1990s to mid-2000s was followed by a dip around 2008-2012, suggesting improved prevention systems.

Sexual assault shows clear increase post-2015, likely reflecting greater reporting confidence rather than more incidents.

Homicide and kidnapping remain consistently small proportions of total offences.

The data shows clear regional differences, suggesting that local context plays a major role in shaping personal safety outcomes. Beyond geography, personal characteristics also influence vulnerability—so next, we look at gender and age patterns among victims and offenders.

Gender and Age Patterns

Understanding who is most affected by crime is key to prevention. This section explores how gender and age influence victimization rates and offence patterns, revealing which groups are most at risk.

Clear demographic patterns emerge in victimization:

  • Teenage girls face highest sexual assault risk
  • Adults 25-44 experience greatest assault burden
  • Males dominate physical violence offences
  • Females disproportionately affected by sexual assault
  • Robbery peaks in teenage years for both genders
Women aged 18-34 record the highest total offence counts across categories.

The findings highlight that personal safety experiences vary widely across demographics, underscoring the need for targeted prevention and support. Since not all offences involve the same level of threat, we next explore the role of weapons used in personal crimes.

Weapons Used

This section investigates the types of weapons involved in personal offences—ranging from physical force to knives or firearms—to understand the intensity and danger associated with different crimes.

Clear divide between private and public crime environments:

Homes dominate for assault and sexual assault, highlighting domestic violence prevalence.

Public spaces see different patterns:

  • Robbery concentrates in streets, transport, and retail areas
  • Community spaces show moderate assault levels
  • Transport hubs are robbery hotspots

Weapon use adds another dimension to understanding personal safety, indicating not only the presence of violence but its potential severity. To further contextualize these incidents, we now look at where crimes most commonly occur.

Crime Location

Personal safety risks often depend on where offences take place. This section explores the most common settings for crimes—such as homes, public areas, or workplaces—to reveal where people are most vulnerable.

Most violent crimes occur without weapons:

91% of assaults and 99% of sexual assaults involve no weapons.

When weapons are used:

  • Robbery is most weapon-dependent (45% of cases)
  • Knives are the most common weapon type
  • Homicide shows greatest weapon variety
  • Firearms remain relatively rare

Patterns in crime locations highlight how personal safety is influenced by environment and routine activities. Finally, we turn to one of the most telling aspects of personal safety: the relationship between victims and perpetrators.

Relationship Between Victims and Perpetrators

This final section examines the connection between victims and offenders, identifying whether offences occur between strangers, acquaintances, or intimate partners. This perspective reveals the social context behind personal violence.

Understanding the relationship between victims and perpetrators is crucial for developing effective personal safety strategies and support services. The data reveals important patterns about where personal safety risks originate—whether from intimate partners, family members, acquaintances, or strangers—and how these relationships vary by gender.

A significant portion of violence occurs within known relationships rather than from strangers.

Patterns vary significantly by:

  • Gender of both victim and perpetrator
  • Type of offence committed
  • Age groups involved
  • Location of incidents

Understanding these relationships completes the picture of personal safety in Australia—showing that threats often arise not just from strangers, but from within familiar and trusted circles.
