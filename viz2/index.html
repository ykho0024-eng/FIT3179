Personal Safety in Australia

Understanding Violence Patterns Across States and Demographics

Personal safety remains a fundamental aspect of wellbeing. It reflects how secure people feel in their daily lives. This visual narrative explores trends in physical and sexual violence in Australia. We use national survey and crime data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics. Through visualisations, we uncover how personal violence has evolved. We examine variations across states and demographic factors.

National Trends Over Time

This section examines overall trends in personal safety incidents. We look at data across Australia over past decades. Understanding these patterns helps identify safety changes.

Population-adjusted rates mirror total cases. This indicates real behavioral shifts. It is not just demographic changes.

The national trend shows a complex picture. After a period of increase, rates stabilized. This suggests impacts of policy interventions.

National averages provide a broad picture. Safety experiences differ across regions. Let's examine state and territory variations.

State Comparison

States and territories show significant variation. This section explores geographical differences. We examine regional factors affecting safety.

Dramatic variations exist across jurisdictions:

  • Northern Territory: Highest rates consistently
  • Queensland: Spike reflects reporting changes
  • Western Australia: Steady deterioration over time
  • Victoria: Lowest-risk with stable performance
  • ACT: Notable improvement since peak

Beyond geographical differences, offence types matter. They tell an important safety story.

Offence Type Composition

Not all incidents are the same. This section breaks down offence types. We examine prevalence and changes over time.

Assault dominates offences throughout the timeline. It comprises 70-80% of all cases.

Steady growth was followed by a dip. This suggests improved prevention systems.

Sexual assault shows clear increase recently. This likely reflects reporting confidence.

Homicide and kidnapping remain small proportions.

Different groups experience risks differently. Let's examine age and gender patterns.

Gender and Age Patterns

Risks are not evenly distributed. This section examines age and gender. We identify groups facing highest risks.

Clear demographic patterns emerge:

  • Teenage girls: Highest sexual assault risk
  • Adults 25-44: Greatest assault burden
  • Males: Dominate physical violence
  • Females: Disproportionate sexual assault
  • Robbery: Peaks in teenage years
Women aged 18-34 record highest offence counts.

Incident locations matter for understanding risks. Let's examine where crimes occur.

Crime Locations

Location provides important context. This section examines where offences happen. We compare private and public spaces.

Clear divide between environments:

Homes dominate for assault types. This highlights domestic violence prevalence.

Public spaces show different patterns:

  • Robbery in streets and transport
  • Community spaces: moderate assaults
  • Transport hubs: robbery hotspots

Weapon presence reveals threat nature. Let's examine weapon usage patterns.

Weapon Use in Crimes

Weapon use impacts severity. This section examines frequency. We look at weapons in different offences.

Most crimes occur without weapons:

91% of assaults involve no weapons. 99% of sexual assaults too.

When weapons are used:

  • Robbery: Most weapon-dependent
  • Knives: Most common type
  • Homicide: Greatest variety
  • Firearms: Relatively rare
