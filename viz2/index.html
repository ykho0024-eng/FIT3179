Personal Safety in Australia

Understanding Violence Patterns Across States and Demographics

Personal safety remains a fundamental aspect of wellbeing, reflecting how secure people feel in their daily lives and relationships. This visual narrative explores trends in physical and sexual violence in Australia, using national survey and crime data from the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS). Through a series of visualisations, we uncover how experiences of personal violence have evolved over time, vary across states, and differ by gender, relationship, and demographic factors.

National Overview — How Has Personal Safety Changed Over Time?

Trends in total violence prevalence from 1993 to 2024.

The data reveals three distinct phases in Australia's personal safety landscape over the past three decades.

2008-2013: Significant Improvement
From 2008 to 2013, offence rates fell quite noticeably. This positive trend could be attributed to improved policing strategies, new legislation, or broader social improvements that contributed to enhanced community safety.

2013-2019: Relative Stability
Between 2013 and 2019, offence levels remained fairly steady with only minor fluctuations, suggesting a period of consolidation in personal safety measures.

Post-2019: Concerning Increase
After 2019, offences rose sharply, reaching peak levels by 2023-2024. This significant jump could be linked to post-COVID societal impacts, population growth dynamics, or changes in reporting practices and legislation.

Key Insight: The population-adjusted rate follows almost the same trendline as total cases, indicating that changes reflect real behavioral shifts rather than being purely population-driven. Offending levels are rising or falling in substantive terms, not just due to demographic changes.

State Comparison: Geographic Variation

Physical offence rates across Australian states and territories.

The map reveals dramatic variations in personal safety across Australian jurisdictions, with some states showing concerning trends while others demonstrate improvement.

Northern Territory: Persistent Crisis
The Northern Territory consistently shows the highest offence rates, far exceeding national baselines. Rates surpass 450 per 10,000 people in recent years, indicating deep-seated structural challenges requiring targeted interventions.

Queensland: Data Reporting Change
The apparent sharp rise in Queensland's recorded offence rate between 2022 and 2024 does not indicate an actual crime surge. Instead, it reflects a data availability change, as assault data for Queensland was not published until 2022. Earlier figures therefore significantly under-represented total offences.

Western Australia: Steady Deterioration
Western Australia shows concerning continuous growth, with offence rates almost doubling over 25 years to reach 173 per 10,000 in 2024. This trend suggests systemic issues that have developed over the long term.

Australian Capital Territory: Improvement Story
The ACT stands out as one of the few improving regions, with crime rates peaking around 2007 then decreasing steadily to settle at lower levels by 2024.

Victoria: Consistent Performance
Victoria remains the lowest-risk state, with relatively minor fluctuations and stable long-term performance, suggesting effective ongoing safety measures.

The map uses normalized color scaling to enable visual comparison across different offence types, with each crime category converted to a common 0-10 scale based on expected ranges.

Offence Type Composition from 1995-2024

Breakdown of physical offences by type over time

Due to unpublished data of assault in some states and years, physical offences are grouped as Assault and Other Physical Offences (homicide, kidnapping) for data consistency.

Assault Dominance
Assault consistently makes up the vast majority of recorded offences, accounting for over 70-80% of all cases across the entire timeline.

Historical Trends
From the mid-1990s to mid-2000s, total offences steadily increased, driven mostly by assault growth. A notable dip around 2008-2012 suggests improvements in prevention and reporting systems.

Sexual Assault: Rising Awareness
While much smaller than assault, sexual assault numbers have clearly risen over time, especially after 2015. This likely indicates greater reporting confidence and societal awareness rather than necessarily more incidents.

Recent Developments
After 2013, levels remained relatively stable before a sharp increase post-2020, likely tied to post-pandemic effects or reporting changes.

Serious but Rare Crimes
Homicide and kidnapping/abduction remain consistently tiny proportions of total offences, though their serious nature demands continued attention.

Gender Comparison — Differences in Experiences

Age and gender distribution of victims across offence types

This population pyramid compares men's and women's experiences of physical and sexual assault by age group, revealing distinct vulnerability patterns across different life stages.

Youth Vulnerability
Teenage girls face the highest risk of sexual violence, while adults aged 25-44—especially women—experience the greatest overall burden of assault.

Gender Divisions
Males dominate physical and robbery-related offences, whereas females are disproportionately affected by sexual assault across all age groups.

Key Demographic Insights:

1. Young People at Highest Sexual Assault Risk
Females aged 10-17 have the highest number of sexual assault cases (12,109)—more than any other age or gender group. Crime prevention and support efforts clearly need to prioritize teenagers, particularly adolescent girls.

2. Assault Peaks in Early Adulthood
For both males and females, assault cases rise sharply from 18 to 44 years, reaching highest levels between 25-34 years. This suggests adults in their working and parenting years face the most physical violence.

3. Robbery as Youth Crime
Robbery peaks in teenagers (10-17 years) for both genders, indicating offending or victimization is strongly linked to adolescence. Numbers drop steadily with age.

4. Total Burden on Young Women
Females aged 18-34 record a total of 64,132 offences compared to 42,484 for males in the same range, suggesting women in early adulthood face high vulnerability across multiple offence categories.

Crime Distribution by Location

Geographic patterns of offences across different locations

This interactive visualization shows how crime cases are distributed across various locations for different offence types, revealing important patterns about where offences most commonly occur.

Private vs. Public Crime Environments
The data reveals a clear divide between private and public crime environments, suggesting the need for different prevention strategies: domestic safety interventions for homes, and situational crime prevention for public spaces.

Location-Specific Patterns:

1. Domestic Settings Dominate Violent Crime
Assault and sexual assault are overwhelmingly concentrated in dwellings (homes or residential settings), suggesting personal violence is more likely to occur in domestic or familiar environments rather than public spaces.

2. Robbery Linked to Public Spaces
Street/footpath, transport, and retail locations show high robbery counts, reflecting opportunistic or confrontational robberies occurring in transit and commercial areas.

3. Moderate Risk in Community Spaces
Parks, social venues, and other public community locations show mid-range levels of assault and sexual assault, indicating risks in nightlife and social gathering environments.

4. Transport and Retail Hotspots
Robbery and assault spike in transport hubs and retail/commercial environments, pointing to the need for security measures in public-facing areas.

Linking Weapons to Crimes

Use of weapon in commission of offence

This Sankey diagram illustrates the relationship between weapon types and offence categories, revealing crucial patterns in how different weapons are associated with various crimes.

Weapon-Free Violence Dominates
Most violent crimes in Australia are committed without formal weapons, relying on physical force or intimidation instead. This pattern is particularly strong in assault (91% no weapon) and sexual assault (99% no weapon).

Robbery: The Weapon-Dependent Crime
Robbery stands out as the most weapon-driven offence, with 45% of cases involving weapons. This reflects the intimidation requirements of this crime type.

Weapon-Specific Insights:

1. Knives: Most Common Weapon
Knives consistently appear as the leading weapon across violent offences, especially in robbery (27%) and homicide (25%), followed by blunt objects in assault-related crimes.

2. Sexual Assault: Rarely Weaponized
Virtually all sexual assault cases (99%) involve no weapon, suggesting offenders rely more on coercion, manipulation, or power imbalance rather than physical tools.

3. Homicide: Greatest Weapon Variety
Homicide shows the most diverse weapon use, with knives (25%) and firearms (12%) far more common than in other offences. Interestingly, nearly half (44%) still occur without weapons.

4. Firearms: Relatively Rare
Firearms remain relatively uncommon across all offence types, appearing most frequently in homicide (12%) but playing minor roles in other crimes.

These weapon patterns provide crucial intelligence for law enforcement strategies and public safety initiatives, highlighting where specific interventions might be most effective.
